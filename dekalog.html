Description

First, I must stress describing something so uniquely special is something I feel I'm not up to task, especially in such a terse form. So, I apologize in advance for any incoherencies brought about by my utter amazement by this series.

Dekalog is loosely based on the ten commandments, but most importantly, should not be viewed through the prism of them. Each of the ten one-hour parts deals with one central moral issue, and branches, just like in real life, into multitude of others. The plot is set in 1989 Warsaw and the characters are mostly tenants of a single apartment block. Episodes and characters are not directly connected, rather much more subtly - through ideas.

This series stands alone in its ability to channel abstract and philosophical questions through unbelievably vivid characters. The non-judgmental love and compassion Kieslowski has for his characters is breathtaking. Had it not been for this relationship, these deeply flawed people would have been left on their own, wandering the seas of misanthropy; like in so many works these days. What this achieves is something that is preciously rare - a real connection with the characters and with bits of ourselves in them. Combined with the oxymoronic abstractness of real world situations, this makes for some serious thinking. Thinking that is, if you are lucky, best shared with someone, or otherwise, done introspectively; like its done by so many Dekalog characters.

It should not be watched in one sitting, rather, ideally, one episode per day. This way you can fully appreciate the unfathomably vast palette of feelings and thoughts this work of art brings out in you; and it has a dazzlingly remarkable way of doing that even without your knowledge.

An essential piece of cinematography. No, an essential work of art.
